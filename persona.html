Exploring future state mobile dashboards for managing online identities and documents

Persona is on a mission to build the web’s most secure and convenient digital passport. I worked with one of the co-founders, Charles Yeh, to create future dashboard interfaces for Persona’s users. My role as the designer was to understand the product on the user’s end and develop the information architecture, user flows, and wireframes.

Introduction

As Persona finds its product market fit in identity verification as a service, users will need a way to manage and control where their personal information is being shared. The user dashboard serves as a launching point for frequent actions and access to information. So how can we make a platform for managing digital identities trustworthy and convenient to people who feel overwhelmed and skeptical towards the security of their digital identities?


Design Objectives

  1. Allow quick management of personal information shared and sites with granted access
  2. Increase trust through enabling users to effortlessly navigate through content and appoint trusted secondary users to manage their account

RESEARCH & SYNTHESIS

To understand the product and how the team got to this point, I annotated flows of current concepts. To understand the users, I looked into existing research on digital security and compared competing identity verification management apps and their corresponding reviews.

TEST & REFINE

After sketching and testing quick walkthroughs with friends to understand how content should flow, I created basic wireframes to focus on key functions, layout, and hierarchy.

FINAL PROTOTYPE

I applied visual design according to Persona’s style guide and created a clickable prototype on Figma to visualize interactions. Next steps would be to test the prototype with users to see whether the design objectives were achieved when compared to the current user dashboard, and iterate based on the findings.

KEY TAKEAWAYS (so far)
  1. Use your design network. Being an only designer is hard — reach out to other designers for feedback and get help when you can. Do the same for others in return.

  2. Be specific about the feedback you want. Having a clear direction for what you’re looking for during meetings and critiques saves time and builds trust.

  3. Learn to live with feeling uncertain. Know that you’re doing your best given what you have. Accept that the design process isn’t linear and straightforward despite what the timeline says. This project benefited from taking multiple steps back to continuously test my assumptions and iterate on new findings.
